# Winston's "How to Speak" — Complete Framework

Source: MIT IAP Lecture, January 2018 (final version). Patrick Winston (1943-2019), Ford Professor of AI at MIT. Given annually for 40+ years. Companion book: *Make It Clear* (MIT Press, 2020).

**Core thesis:** Quality = f(Knowledge, Practice, Talent) — in decreasing importance. Speaking is a learnable skill. "Your success in life will be determined largely by your ability to speak, your ability to write, and the quality of your ideas. In that order."

---

## Opening — The Empowerment Promise

- **Start with a promise**, not a joke. Tell the audience what they will know or be able to do at the end that they can't now. This gives them a reason to pay attention.
- **Never start with a joke.** The audience isn't warmed up; a failed joke poisons everything.
- Write a **menu on the board** (or show a roadmap slide) to signal the talk has started and orient the audience.
- Optionally anchor with a shocking fact.

## The Big Four Heuristics

Winston says each speaker should develop their own set, but these are his four:

### 1. Cycle
Return to your main argument 3-5 times, each from a different angle. ~20% of the audience is "fogged out" at any moment. First pass establishes the schema, subsequent passes build long-term memory, the final pass indexes key points. For short talks (<10 min), 1-2 cycles max.

### 2. Build a Fence / Near Miss
Distinguish your idea from similar ones. Show what it is NOT using adjacent-but-wrong examples. The arch example: Winston shows something that looks like an arch but isn't, to clarify what makes an actual arch. This comes from AI concept learning — you understand a category by seeing its boundaries.

### 3. Verbal Punctuation
Structural markers that let distracted listeners "jump back on the bus." Use numbering, progress statements, recaps. Summarize key points every ~5 minutes (average adult attention span drops off).

### 4. Calibrated Questions
Ask questions that are neither too easy (insulting) nor too hard (embarrassing). Wait **7 seconds** for responses. The pause feels uncomfortable but is necessary. Questions activate engagement and keep the audience's language processor working on your material.

## Tools: Time and Place

- **11 AM** is optimal (no morning grogginess, no post-lunch food coma)
- **Well-lit room** — dim lights cause drowsiness. Always bright.
- **Case the room** beforehand — eliminate surprises with equipment, lighting, sightlines
- **Match room to crowd** — packed small room beats sparse large one. Fill more than half the seats.
- Avoid post-lunch time slots

## Tools: Board, Props, Slides

### Board (for teaching)
- Writing speed matches audience absorption speed — natural pacing mechanism
- **Empathetic mirroring**: Mirror neurons activate when audience watches you write. They feel as if they're writing. Cannot happen with slides.
- Provides a **hand target** — natural place for gestures
- Write a recurring logo/symbol capturing main concepts

### Props
- Physical objects are the most powerful engagement tool
- People remember the prop long after forgetting specific arguments
- Visual perception is the most effective engagement channel

### Slide Rules
- Too many slides, too many words — always
- **24-40pt font** minimum
- **Never read slides aloud** — "One language processor": audiences can read OR listen, not both simultaneously. Text-heavy slides force a choice and they lose both.
- **No bullet points** — use plain text or images
- No clip art, backgrounds, logos, titles (speak the title instead)
- **No laser pointers** — use on-slide arrows. "There is a special circle in hell for those who use laser pointers."
- Use striking images worth a thousand words
- **Hapax legomenon**: Reserve ONE dense/text-heavy slide per presentation max. Pause and give time to read.
- Remove everything non-essential

## Rules of Engagement

Ask audience to close laptops and put away phones. "One language processor" — they can't read/type and listen simultaneously. This sets the social contract.

## Informing vs. Teaching

**Informing** = promise + inspiration + how to think. Deliver on your promise, show genuine passion.

**Teaching** goes deeper. Provide:
1. **Stories** the audience should know
2. **Questions** they should ask about those stories
3. **Methods** for analyzing stories
4. **Approaches** to combining/synthesizing
5. **Ways** to evaluate reliability

This is what distinguishes teaching from information transfer.

## Persuading

### Oral Exams
- **Situate your work** in 5 minutes: what you did, its importance, place it in context
- Practice with non-expert friends willing to **aggressively question** you

### Job Talks
- First 5 minutes must show: (1) **Vision** — a problem + your novel approach, (2) **Concrete progress** — steps completed, results
- Use a **Contributions slide** (not Conclusions) — stays up during Q&A

### Getting Famous — Winston's Star (5 S's)
To make ideas as famous as they deserve:

1. **Symbol** — visual icon for your concept
2. **Slogan** — memorable phrase, linguistic handle
3. **Surprise** — something unexpected, challenges misconceptions
4. **Salient idea** — the most memorable detail (not necessarily most important)
5. **Story** — the journey: obstacles, failures, breakthroughs, relief

## How to Stop

### Final Slide
**Contributions slide**, not conclusions. Lists specific original contributions. Shows collaborators (but earlier in the talk, not last). Never end on "Questions?" or "Thank You" or a URL.

### Three Closing Techniques

1. **Joke** — Humor lands better at the end. By now the audience knows you. "People will leave feeling like they had fun the whole time." (Distinct from "never START with a joke.")
2. **Salute** — Compliment the audience without thanking them. Express how much you value the occasion/audience. Bill Clinton style.
3. **Benediction** — Formal, ceremonial close. "God bless you, and God bless America."

### Never
- "Thank you" — signals audience endured your talk out of politeness
- "Thank you for listening" — implies they were trapped
- "Any questions?" — anticlimactic whimper
- Deliver on your opening promise in the closing — full circle

## Contested Points

These rules are genuinely debated. The skill should acknowledge both sides:

- **"No joke" opening**: Safe for non-comedians (risk-reward is asymmetric). Professional speakers with tested material are the exception.
- **"No thank you" ending**: TED eliminated it, but many professional contexts expect it. Compromise: deliver strong close FIRST, then brief acknowledgment.
- **Board > slides**: Most modern venues lack chalkboards. The slide rules (minimal text, images) are well-supported by cognitive science. The board preference is harder to implement.
