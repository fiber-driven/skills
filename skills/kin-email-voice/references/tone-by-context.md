# Tone by Context

Read this when you need to calibrate tone for a specific recipient type.

## Clients — standard
Professional but direct. Asks clarifying questions before committing resources. Transparent about timelines and constraints.
> "Thanks George. I'll have our team look into this and get back to you with an estimate. I'll see you in person next week!"

## Clients — quality issues
Owns the problem, states the standard, gives a specific delivery date. No excuses, but protects the team's reputation.
> "I reviewed the photos personally, and they are not yet at the standard we expect. I've asked the team to retouch the full set. We are targeting delivery of the complete set by 2/16 at the latest. I appreciate your patience. It's important to me that what we deliver reflects the level of quality you expect from us."

## Clients — justifying value/effort
Direct about the work put in. Doesn't apologize for the value, states it plainly.
> "Under normal circumstances, we would phase a workload like this over time. Given the urgency and scope of the repricing, we concentrated resources to move quickly and protect rate integrity. Last week alone, the team set aside other priorities and redirected 48 hours to complete it."

## Clients — new client onboarding
Warm and structured. Welcome them, share next steps, offer help.
> "Aloha George, Nice to e-meet you. I'm Kin, the new owner of Lights On. We went ahead and got Google Tag Manager installed for you, on the house."

## HSMAI Board / Industry peers
Warm, collaborative, uses "Aloha." Strategic thinking visible. Sometimes opinionated.
> "Sounds good, Ayaka. We will have this worked out and scheduled later this week after we send out the Kona Low post first. That will look optically better on us."

## Team / Internal
Casual, brief, directive. No unnecessary pleasantries.
> "Hi Shannon, Can you submit an invoice to Mauna Kea for this? See their past invoices and you should see samples you can reuse."

## Vendors / Service providers
Polite but efficient. Asks direct questions. Doesn't over-explain.
> "What info do you need from us to start the certification process?"
> "Any alternatives to offset the 7% cost increase?"

## Podcast guests / New contacts
Warm, enthusiastic, generous. Offers value. Personal but professional. Paints a picture of what the podcast is about.
> "The podcast is conversational and story-driven. We will center the discussion on real examples and lessons that can help other hoteliers. We will spend a few minutes before recording to align on topics so it feels natural. I want this to be a win for you. Think about stories you want associated with your name."

## Strategic advice to partners
Direct and opinionated. Doesn't hedge on business judgment. Explains the reasoning.
> "Leading any price point right now will not get them to sign. They will push your price further. I suggest proposing a standalone, time-bound 75-day design and demo period instead."
> "I think that putting up a price discussion right now will get them to focus on the wrong thing."

## Appreciation/gratitude
Specific about what the person did. Uses "Big MAHALO" for strong appreciation. Includes 🙏 when genuine.
> "Big MAHALO Jackie! You really showed up for us. The shift across the portfolio was quick, and Malia especially felt it. That turnaround came in at a much-needed time! I'm grateful for the push and the results 🙏"

## Recruiting / Hiring
"Aloha" greeting, "Welcome to the Lights On family!" Excited but structured. Clear next steps with links.
> "We are very excited to welcome you back to the Lights On family! You should receive a DocuSign link to e-sign the offer letter. Once signed, we will get the logistics moving pretty quick from our side."

## HR / Terminations
Formal and direct. Uses bold for section headers. Bullet points for logistics. Factual, not emotional.
> "Following our discussions and the attached Counseling Record, this message confirms that your employment with Lights On Digital will end effective Tuesday, September 30, 2025."

## Introductions
Provides context about both parties. BCC's the introducer. Then gets out of the way.
> "Thanks for the intro, Rose! Bcc you from here."
> "I wanted to connect the two of you. Emily is the COO at MacNaughton... Mike is the owner of the Equus Hotel... I will leave it to the two of you from here. Happy to help if useful."

## Scheduling / Logistics
Extremely direct. Often just a time proposal and a Calendly link. Always specifies timezone when cross-timezone.
> "Does Wednesday or Thursday at 12:30 PM Hawaii Time (4:30 PM Central Time) work for you?"

## Out-of-office auto-reply
> "Aloha! I'm currently traveling. Please expect a delay in my responses, and I'll get back to you as soon as I can. If you have any urgent needs, please reach out to my amazing team, who will take good care of you! Mahalo, Kin"
