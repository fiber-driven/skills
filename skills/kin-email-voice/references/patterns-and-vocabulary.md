# Patterns and Vocabulary

Read this for detailed language patterns, structural habits, and vocabulary reference.

## Structure Patterns

**Short paragraphs.** Most are 1-2 sentences. Three sentences in a paragraph is the upper bound.

**Bullet points for multiple items.** When there are 2+ action items, next steps, or pieces of information, use bullets. Kin bolds key phrases within bullets for scannability.

**Numbered lists for sequential items or answering multiple questions.** When replying to someone's numbered points:
```
1) HMSA - I believe so. The google doc I shared with you should have the detail.
2) yes 4-day work week is still the goal
3) office has parking for $150/month, not covered by lights on.
```

**Inline response style.** When replying point-by-point to someone's formal questions or list, uses `[Kin]` prefix:
```
- Timeline: when will be the potential recording date?

[Kin] Recording can be scheduled at your convenience using our booking link.
```

**@mentions in group threads.** Uses `@Name` to direct attention to specific people in group emails.

**Calendly links.** Proactively shares scheduling links rather than going back and forth on times. Two common formats:
- Bare URL on its own line: `https://calendly.com/kin-lightson/30min`
- Labeled link: `Podcast scheduling link <https://calendly.com/kin-lightson/podcast-interview>`

**Loom videos for complex explanations.** Instead of writing long explanations, records a Loom and shares the link. "See this Loom video" or "A quick video walkthrough of the proposal (link)"

**Links with labels in parentheses.** For sharing multiple resources:
```
- A quick video walkthrough of the proposal (link)
- Detailed proposal (link)
- Dale's branding work samples (link)
```

**P.S. for secondary asks.** Uses P.S. naturally for afterthoughts, acknowledgments, or lighter follow-ups:
> "P.S. Thanks again for the introduction to Jessie at Guest OS. We've found some solid ways to work together."

**Screenshots.** Frequently attaches CleanShot screenshots for visual context instead of describing things in text.

**Multiple recipients addressed separately.** Name as its own line, then content:
```
Audra,
Can you share what you learned from Bill.com regarding the payment issue?

Shannon,
Audra told me about a payment issue for the following bill. Can you work with Audra to figure this out?
```

## Natural Language Patterns

**Contractions always.** I'll, we'll, can't, don't, won't, it'll. Never "I will" or "we will" in conversational emails. Exception: formal/HR emails may use full forms.

**"Let me know"** — Kin's go-to soft close for action items. Sometimes "Let me know, thanks!" or "Let me know if you have any questions."

**"Stay tuned."** — For things in progress. Often its own sentence/paragraph.

**"Looking forward to..."** — Genuine, not performative. Variations: "Looking forward to it!", "Looking forward to our recording next week!", "Looking forward to connecting!"

**"Can you..."** — Direct asks, not passive constructions. Never "Would it be possible for you to..."

**"Thanks!"** — With exclamation mark, not period. Sometimes "Thanks as always!"

**"Awesome!"** — Frequent opener for positive replies. "Awesome! Can you fill out this onboarding form..."

**"Talk soon!"** — Casual sign-off for people he'll be speaking with.

**"On top of my head"** — Kin's version of "off the top of my head." Don't correct this — it's how he writes.

**"Will be great if..."** / **"It'll be great to..."** — Soft suggestion framing. More natural than "It would be great if..."

**"Let's chat more on this!"** — For topics that need a call rather than email back-and-forth.

**"on the house"** — For complimentary work. "We went ahead and got Google Tag Manager installed for you, on the house."

**"e-meet"** for virtual first introductions. "Nice to e-meet you, Ken!"

**"Big MAHALO"** — For strong appreciation to Hawaii/local contacts.

## Humor and Emoji

Humor is used sparingly but naturally. Smiley faces `:)`, winky face `;)` (rare), laughing emoji `😂`, prayer hands `🙏`, and light self-deprecation. Never forced.
> "Can we start 10 mins later today? I'm waiting for my nanny to take over the kid :)"
> "Dubai sounds fun! The UAE is getting all the new attractions. Disneyland Abu Dhabi is underway... Maybe a Kualoa Ranch there at some point? :)"
> "Honestly, I haven't looked into our email marketing situation... :)"

**Emoji frequency:** `:)` is most common. `😂` for genuinely funny situations. `🙏` for deep gratitude. `🔗` occasionally for link labels. Never in formal/first-contact emails.

**Exclamation marks.** More frequent than typical CEO emails but never excessive. Often on: "Thanks!", "Awesome!", one enthusiasm statement per email. Multiple exclamation marks are fine across an email, but never back-to-back ("Great!! Awesome!!").

## Honesty About Mistakes

Kin is transparent when things go wrong. No spin, just states the fact and moves on:
> "Got the wrong email yesterday..."
> "Actually, I realized that Kaycee's payroll wasn't included in this processing due to the original termination date."
> "[forgot to put a date]"

## Delegation Pattern

Kin frequently delegates by CC'ing team members with clear context:
> "Hi GlassView team, I have a conflict during this time. Please reach out to my marketing team (Emanuel & Serena) if you want to schedule a demo."
> "Please work with Jemmerio and Conor on my side for Whaler's Cove. They have more context to the situation. Best to hop on a call with them."

When introducing a team member into a thread:
> "I'll start bringing in Itzel from my social media team for future communication. She's amazing and has been helping me with the HSMAI content."

## Introduction Pattern

When introducing two people:
1. Address both by name
2. One sentence of context about each person (role + why they're relevant)
3. State why the connection makes sense
4. Step back: "I will leave it to the two of you from here. Happy to help if useful."

When receiving an intro:
> "Thanks for the intro, Rose! Bcc you from here."
Always BCC the introducer out of the thread.

## Follow-up Pattern

Direct and to the point:
> "Just following up on my email below. Did you get a chance to review the onboarding form for API access?"

No guilt trips, no "I know you're busy," no passive pressure.

- Video walkthrough via Loom comes first
- Labeled links for each deliverable
- Brief framing sentence, then links, then soft close
> "Following up from our call last week, we want to share a more detailed breakdown of the proposal:"
